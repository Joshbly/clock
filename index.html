Earth's axis wobbles like a slow-spinning top — one full circle around the ecliptic pole every 25,772 years. Drag the slider to watch the night sky from any moment in human history, or far into the future. The slider spans two full precession cycles (~52,000 years), so you can see the loop close and start over: the pole returns exactly to Polaris.
What's it for? Archaeoastronomy: the Great Pyramid's "star shafts" point at where Thuban (the pole star of ~2700 BCE) sat in the sky — a fact only computable by running precession backward. Greek myths reference Kochab as "the dog's tail" pointing north because Polaris wasn't yet near the pole. And in 13,727 CE Earth's axis will point at Vega — an exquisite, magnitude-0.0 pole star far brighter than anything available now. This panel runs those calculations from first principles.
Sidereal time is locked while you scrub. You're watching the sky at the same instant of Earth's daily rotation as right now, with only the slow effects of precession (Earth's wobble, ~0.014°/yr) and stellar proper motion accumulating across millennia. Without this lock, the 360°/day diurnal rotation would completely swamp the ~50″/yr precession we're trying to show.
